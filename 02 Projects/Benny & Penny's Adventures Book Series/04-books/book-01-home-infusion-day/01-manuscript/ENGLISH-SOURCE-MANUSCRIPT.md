## Page 05 - Penny’s home infusion day

Hi, I’m Penny.

I love coloring, playing, and spending time with my best friend, Benny Bear.

Today is a home infusion day. Nurse Ivy is coming to visit me at home.

I feel excited to see her. I also have questions. Sometimes I even feel a little nervous.

---

## Page 06 - Big feelings are okay

That is okay, Benny Bear reminds me.

Big feelings can visit all at once.

When I feel nervous, I can tell a grown-up. I can hold Benny close. I can take a slow breath.

I do not have to be brave all by myself.

---

## Page 07 - Nurse Ivy arrives

Knock, knock!

“Hi, Penny,” says Nurse Ivy as she kneels beside me. “I’m happy to see you.”

Nurse Ivy has a warm smile and a calm voice. She lets me look at her bag and asks, “What would help you feel cozy today?”

“Benny,” I say. “And a story.”

“Perfect,” she says.

---

## Page 08 - Penny’s comfort plan

Nurse Ivy says, “We can take one small step at a time. You can ask questions whenever you want.”

I practice saying, “What is that?” and “Can you tell me what happens next?”

Nurse Ivy listens to every question.

My feelings matter here.

---

## Page 09 - Nervous feelings

I look at the medicine bag and the soft tubing.

“Sometimes this part makes me nervous,” I whisper.

Nurse Ivy nods. “That makes sense. New or hard things can feel big.”

Benny Bear sits on my lap. His soft paws remind me that I am safe and loved.

---

## Page 10 - Brave breaths

We make a brave-breath plan.

In ... one, two, three.

Out ... one, two, three.

I squeeze Benny’s paw and look at something happy in my room.

Each slow breath makes my body feel a little calmer.

---

## Page 11 - Benny Bear

Benny Bear is my best friend.

He is soft, cuddly, and always ready for a hug.

He never rushes me. He stays close when I feel wiggly, worried, proud, or sleepy.

---

## Page 12 - Our comfort team

Before Nurse Ivy begins, Benny and I choose our comfort team: a cozy blanket, a favorite story, and a grown-up hand to hold.

“We make a great team,” I tell Benny.

His little bow looks extra brave today.

---

## Page 13 - What an infusion is

Nurse Ivy explains, “An infusion is one way your body can get medicine.”

“The medicine travels through a tiny, soft bendy straw called a catheter,” she says. “I follow the plan your doctor made just for you.”

“Oh,” I say. “So the medicine knows where to go.”

“That’s right,” Nurse Ivy says.

---

## Page 14 - Questions are welcome

Some words can sound big at first.

Big words are okay - I can always ask what they mean.

I ask, “What is the tubing for?”

Nurse Ivy shows me in a simple way and tells me what she is doing.

Learning about something helps it feel less mysterious.

---

## Page 15 - Getting ready

When it is time to get ready, Nurse Ivy talks me through my own care plan.

She helps make my space clean, calm, and cozy.

I can choose where Benny sits. I can listen to my story. I can ask for a pause to take a breath.

---

## Page 16 - Penny does a hard thing

Some parts may feel quick and uncomfortable. I can tell Nurse Ivy how I feel.

I take a breath. I hold Benny. I squeeze a caring hand.

Then the hard part is over.

“I did it,” I say.

“You did,” Nurse Ivy says. “You did something hard with your whole brave heart.”

---

## Page 17 - Meet Polly Pump

Next, Nurse Ivy introduces me to Polly Pump.

“Polly is a helpful pump,” she says. “She gives your medicine a steady little push.”

Polly has buttons, lights, and a very important job.

“Hello, Penny,” says Polly. “I will keep working while you rest, play, or listen to a story.”

---

## Page 18 - Polly’s job

Polly works at the pace in my care plan while Nurse Ivy keeps an eye on everything.

I do not have to watch every minute.

I can color a picture, cuddle Benny, or close my eyes for a rest.

My helpers are doing their jobs, and I can be a kid.

---

## Page 19 - Meet Danny Dial-a-Flow

Then I meet Danny Dial-a-Flow.

Danny is connected with the tubing and helps keep the flow at the setting Nurse Ivy chooses for my plan.

Danny has a dial, but I do not need to turn it.

That is Nurse Ivy’s job.

---

## Page 20 - Danny’s job

Danny helps the medicine move at a steady pace - not too fast and not too slow for me.

Nurse Ivy checks the setup and makes sure everything is comfortable.

“Thank you, Danny,” I say.

Danny gives a tiny proud wiggle.

---

## Page 21 - Meet Ellie Infusion Ball

Last, I meet Ellie Infusion Ball.

Ellie is round, gentle, and quiet. She holds medicine before it travels through the tubing.

Ellie does not need batteries or loud beeps.

She just keeps helping, little by little.

---

## Page 22 - Ellie’s job

Ellie reminds me that quiet helpers can be powerful too.

While Ellie works, I can play, watch a show, draw, rest, or have a snack when it is right for me.

Every home infusion day can look a little different, because every child has their own plan.

---

## Page 23 - Helpers together

Polly, Danny, and Ellie each have a different job.

Nurse Ivy knows how they work together, and she watches my care closely.

Benny stays right beside me.

I am surrounded by helpers.

---

## Page 24 - Still Penny’s day

Time passes.

I color. I listen to my favorite story. I laugh when Benny falls over in a silly heap.

The medicine has time to do its job.

Home infusion day is still a day for love, comfort, and being me.

---

## Page 25 - Brave today

When Nurse Ivy says we are all finished for today, I feel proud.

I had questions. I had nervous feelings. I took breaths. I let people help me.

“I was brave today,” I say.

Benny Bear gives me the biggest cuddle.

---

## Page 26 - Closing reassurance

Nurse Ivy smiles.

“Being brave does not mean you never feel nervous,” she says. “It means you keep going with love and support.”

I look at Benny, my comfort team, and my cozy room.

I can do hard things.

I am safe.

I am not alone.

## Page 32 - For Parents & Caregivers

# For Parents & Caregivers

You know your child best.

Before, during, and after an infusion, invite honest feelings without needing to fix them right away. Simple choices can help a child feel more involved: choosing a comfort item, a story, a favorite activity, or a cozy place to sit.

Use the words your child’s own care team uses. Keep explanations honest, gentle, and age-appropriate. Celebrate coping, not perfection:

- “You took a breath.”
- “You told us how you felt.”
- “You asked a good question.”
- “You let us help.”

This book is a conversation starter, not medical instruction. Follow your child’s individual care plan and contact your child’s healthcare team with questions or concerns.

With warmth,

**Michelle Marie Pinto, RN**
